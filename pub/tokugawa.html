Tokugawa Era

Japanese Societal Structure

The Tokugawa period marks the first time we see a modern societal structure within a newly unified Japan. Based on Chinese Confucianism, society under the regime was broken into four classes and arranged hierarchically. 1

Outside Of The Structure

While a majority of the population fell within the four categories outlined in Tokugawa social order, a chunk of the population were considered outcasts. The two primary groups to be considered outside society were:

Ainu

Eta

All outcasts were physically relegated to the outskirts of society by placing them at the outskirts of cities 5

In addition, religion, like its priests, were highly regulated. All citizens were required to be registered to the temples, which were also regulated by number and location. The goal for these rules was to prevent the temples becoming too powerful as they did in the past to challenge the Tokugawa authority. The restrictions influenced foreign policy as well. 6

Ainu

The Ainu (located in present day Hokkaido) were a group of aboriginal inhabitants of the Japanese Islands. Culturally separate from southern Honshu, they mainly stayed separate from the Japanese. They were neither a member of Japanese society or considered "barbaric" foreigners.

Eta

Eta (meaning "much filth") were a group of outcasts that didn't fit within the societal class structure within the Tokugawa order. This group of people primarily had jobs which were "deemed unclean by mainstream society." This included burials, executions, and the handling of animal carcasses. 8 These outcasts will be referred to later as the Buraku and will become more prominent in Post-Pacific War Japan. 9

The Dutch Ruled Trade From Europe... Because Religion

For Tokugawa Japan, trade became a delicate walk. The Spanish and Portuguese wanted to sell religion with their material goods. The idea behind Christianity proved disruptive to the ideas established by Shinto and Budhism, which were already regulated by the regime. In 1637-38, Catholic peasants staged a rebellion which failed. 10

The Dutch, after Spain, England, and Portugal abandoned trade, remained, deciding only to focus on goods rather than spreading religion. 11
