Outside Of The Structure

While a majority of the population fell within the four categories outlined in Tokugawa social order, a chunk of the population were considered outcasts. The two primary groups to be considered outside society were:

Ainu

Eta

All outcasts were physically relegated to the outskirts of society by placing them at the outskirts of cities (Gordon 18)

In addition, religion, like its priests, were highly regulated. All citizens were required to be registered to the temples, which were also regulated by number and location. The goal for these rules was to prevent the temples becoming too powerful as they did in the past to challenge the Tokugawa authority. The restrictions influenced foreign policy as well.

Ainu

The Ainu (located in present day Hokkaido) were a group of aboriginal inhabitants of the Japanese Islands. Culturally separate from southern Honshu, they mainly stayed separate from the Japanese. They were neither a member of Japanese society or considered "barbaric" foreigners.

Close

Eta

Eta (meaning "much filth") were a group of outcasts that didn't fit within the societal class structure within the Tokugawa order. This group of people primarily had jobs which were "deemed unclean by mainstream society." This included burials, executions, and the handling of animal carcasses. (Gordon 18)
