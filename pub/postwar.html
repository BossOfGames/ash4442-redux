Post-War and Occupation

Japan under occupation provided a dramatic change, both in the roles of people and in government.

The MacArthur Constitution

MacArthur's committee of occupation officials, in the winter of 1946, created a new constitution. The most notable changes are as follows:

Fundamental Human Rights

Like the United States constitution, fundamental human rights were established. Including freedoms of speech, assembly, and religion.1

No Absolute Monarch

Downgraded from an absolute monarch, the constitution repositioned the emperor to "symbol of the State and of the unity of the people."2

Renunciation of War

From Article 9: "Aspiring sincerely to an international peace based on justice and order, the Japanese people forever renounce war as a sovereign right of the nation and the threat or use of force as a mean of settling international disputes. In order to accomplish the aim of the preceding paragraph, land, sea, and air forces, as well as other war potential, will never be maintained. The right of belligerency of the state will not be recognized."3


This has, in recent years, been more lenient as the JSDF (Japanese Self-Defense Force) formed to aid in the protection of the country and UN peacekeeping operations.4

The Cold War Politics

Unfortunately, Japan couldn't escape the affects of the Cold War. This was especially true during the rebuilding process. The communist movement in Japan grew strong, which meant MacArthur and the US needed to course correct before things got out of hand. To do so, he took the following actions.

  1. Sharply scaled back plans to dissolve the former subsidiaries of the zaibatsu combines.
  2. Relinquished all claims to war reparations
  3. Revise new postwar labor laws to outlaw strikes by public employees
  4. Encouraged the creation of a national police force with the Korean outbreak so the US can redirect troops.

In addition to the above actions, others were taken, such as the crackdown on the Japanese Communist Party. Members of which were ousted from both private and public positions on the grounds their political activities were interfering with the occupation. 5

The Perception of Americans

Post-war, Americans had a mixed reception from the Japanese. Ever sine the US-Japan Security Treaty was signed, the United States kept (and still keeps today) bases in Japan to protect and contain Japan. This was very controversial in Japan. They felt that the presence of the US put the country as a target. 6

The Landscape Changes on Inclusion

It is important to note that thanks to America getting directly involved in the reconstruction, sweeping changes in the government and structure is finally allowing oppressed groups footing to fight back and change their status.

Ainu in Post-War Japan

Little has changed in post-war Japan for its native group. Most were disadvantaged as they were during pre-war. "Assimilation was still regarded as the only solution."7 However, the uniqueness of their culture became teh subject of Tourism in Hokkaido. So some opportunity existed, but at a result of continuing prejudice.

It was not until the 1960s when the Ainu challenged and protested the racism towards then. The government started to enact welfare policies for the Ainu, however the policies carried more than welfare. The Ainu were challenging the notion they were a 'dying race' as people started to learn the rituals and arts that represented their culture.8

Finally in 1997, a landmark ruling by the Sapporo District Court classified the Ainu as a indigenous people. Then in the same year, the Ainu Cultural Promotion Act became law. However the laws still excluded them from Japanese citizenship.9

The Burakumin

Coming to Post-War Japan, Matsumoto Jiichiro, the leader of the Suiheisha met with the former movement's leaders to discuss plans on resuming efforts to liberate the buraku. The timing of which was perfect. As discussed in the new constitution, there were guarantees for human rights, unlike previously.10

However, the guarantees in the constitution did not mean everyone played along. In October 1951, a magazine published a story painting a false picture of the buraku. Kyoto city accepted the responsibility of making conditions unfaborable and increased the budget for improvements to ￥45 million. 11

In 1969, a ten year plan was established that contained a lot of programs to resolve the problem. These were the goals of the policy.12

  1. Physical Environment
  2. Social Welfare & Public Health
  3. Promotion of Agriculture, Forestry and Fisheries
  4. Promotion of Small & Medium-Sized Enterprises
  5. Employment Protection & Social Security
  6. Human Rights Protections

Barring local variations, there were three types of programs.

Physical Environment

Improved streets,s chools, clinics and community centers and constructing high-rise appartment blocks to replace old housing.13

System Of Grants

Paid directly to buraku families as a way to ease financial difficulties. For example, a family in Osaka with two children could receive over ￥400,000 annually including one-off payments on marriage or birth of child. 14

Education

Programs within school classroom and outside aimed at changing attitudes among the general public. 15
